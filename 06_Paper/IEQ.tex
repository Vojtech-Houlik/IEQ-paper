\maketitle
\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates whether overall indoor environmental quality (IEQ) satisfaction in classrooms can be predicted from environmental, contextual, occupant, and building-related variables. The analysis uses an open Belgian classroom dataset containing 6{,}834 repeated satisfaction assessments from 321 occupants across primary, secondary, and university classrooms. The original five-point overall IEQ scale is collapsed into three classes: dissatisfied, neutral, and satisfied. Five model families are compared under matched five-fold cross-validation, using macro F1 as the main selection metric because the target distribution is imbalanced. The best single model is an Extra Trees classifier, reaching macro F1 = 0.591, accuracy = 0.723, balanced accuracy = 0.592, and ordinal MAE = 0.315.

The confusion matrices show that satisfied votes are predicted most reliably, while dissatisfied and neutral responses remain harder to separate. Feature-importance and sensitivity analyses suggest that overall IEQ satisfaction is shaped by a combination of environmental, occupant, and contextual factors rather than by one dominant domain alone. Follow-up experiments with complete-case filtering, feature engineering, synthetic class balancing, and primary-school-specific training produce only modest gains and reveal domain shift between educational settings. The results quantify the performance and limits of classroom IEQ prediction and show how such models could support occupant-centered and energy-aware decision support in school buildings.
\end{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} indoor environmental quality; classroom comfort; occupant satisfaction; machine learning; Extra Trees; transfer learning; Belgian classroom dataset \vspace{0.2\baselineskip}

\Reviewed{
\section{Introduction}

Indoor environmental quality (IEQ) describes the combined indoor conditions experienced by occupants, especially thermal comfort, indoor air quality, lighting, and acoustics. In classrooms, these conditions can affect students' comfort, engagement, well-being, and learning \cite{carton2023iaqvec,sadick2017,carton2023primary,yang2020classroomieq,alam2025schoolieq}. These effects are large enough to matter in everyday classroom operation, and they are not limited to obviously uncomfortable rooms. In a field study of 140 fifth-grade classrooms, each 1~L/s per person increase in ventilation within 0.9--7.1~L/s per person was associated with an increase of up to about 0.5\% in mathematics scores. Within the ordinary 20--25~$^\circ$C classroom-temperature range, each 1~$^\circ$C decrease was associated with a further increase of about 0.5--0.6\%. Published school-specific relationships also suggest larger effects under poorer conditions: lowering classroom temperature from 30~$^\circ$C to 20~$^\circ$C can increase performance by about 20\%, and reducing classroom CO$_2$ from 2{,}100~ppm to 900~ppm can improve task speed by about 12\% \cite{haverinen2015testscore,wargocki2019temperature,wargocki2020airquality}. Recent thermal-comfort research reflects this need: PMV and adaptive comfort models are increasingly complemented by personal comfort models that use IoT data and machine learning to predict individual thermal preference \cite{kim2018newparadigm,kim2018,arakawa2022}. These models are also moving into practice: personal-comfort-based control has been tested in occupied buildings, and one commercial system has used machine learning to translate warm/cool feedback into HVAC commands and thermostat schedules \cite{xu2026pcmcontrol,baker2016comfy}. However, this progress still focuses mainly on thermal comfort and HVAC control, not on overall classroom IEQ across air quality, lighting, and acoustics. In practice, classrooms are often monitored through separate indicators such as temperature, CO$_2$, illuminance, and sound level. These separate limits do not show whether students perceive the room as acceptable overall. They also miss operational trade-offs: shading can reduce overheating but lower daylight, and more ventilation can reduce CO$_2$ but affect thermal comfort, noise, and energy use \cite{frontczak2012,heinzerling2013,zhao2023,zhang2023ieqmodels}. This paper therefore focuses on developing a model for predicting overall IEQ satisfaction: how students judge the classroom environment as a whole, rather than as isolated responses to individual IEQ domains. The aim is to support better handling of energy--comfort trade-offs in classroom operation \cite{wong2008,tang2022,roumi2022}.

Several gaps in the IEQ literature motivate this focus. Reviews show that overall IEQ satisfaction is affected by several domains at the same time, but many existing models do not fully capture these interactions and have only limited validation across real buildings \cite{heinzerling2013,zhao2023,roumi2022,zhang2023ieqmodels}. Many models also rely on subjective votes for separate domains, such as thermal, air-quality, visual, or acoustic satisfaction. These inputs are useful for research, but they are not always available in routine classroom monitoring, where decisions must often rely on sensor and context data. Recent sensor-based studies show that monitored environmental data can predict perceived IEQ, including student perceptions in schools \cite{kallio2020,riffelli2022,alam2025schoolieq}. However, classrooms remain less developed than offices or commercial buildings: students have limited control over the indoor environment, may respond differently from adults, and existing studies are difficult to compare or reuse \cite{teli2012,lala2022children,miao2024students,carton2026review}. The open Belgian classroom dataset helps address this gap by linking repeated overall and domain satisfaction votes with environmental, personal, and contextual variables across primary, secondary, and university classrooms \cite{carton2026dataset}. This paper uses that dataset to test how well overall IEQ satisfaction can be predicted from classroom sensor, occupant, and context data, whether the prediction can be personalized, and how well models generalize across educational settings.}

\subsection{Research Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{RQ1: What prediction accuracy can be achieved for IEQ satisfaction using sensor and contextual data?}

    \item \textbf{RQ2: Which sensor, occupant, and contextual variables are most influential to IEQ satisfaction?}

    \item \textbf{RQ3: How many occupant votes are required to achieve effective personalization for individual occupants or specific classrooms?}

    \item \textbf{RQ4: What performance loss occurs when prediction models are transferred across educational settings?}
\end{enumerate}}
\Reviewed{
\subsection{Contribution}

The practical value of this work is to move classroom IEQ assessment from isolated threshold checking toward occupant-centered and energy-aware decision support. Instead of evaluating temperature, CO$_2$, light, or sound separately, the study asks whether available classroom data can indicate when students are likely to perceive the indoor environment negatively as a whole.

\begin{itemize}
    \item It provides a first step toward future energy--comfort optimization by predicting overall IEQ satisfaction instead of treating temperature, CO$_2$, light, and sound as separate thresholds.
    \item It evaluates how well a three-class model can predict overall IEQ satisfaction and identifies where the prediction remains uncertain (RQ1).
    \item It identifies which measured variables are most useful for dashboard warnings and intervention prioritization (RQ2).
    \item It tests whether prediction can be improved by adapting the pooled model to a specific classroom or occupant (RQ3).
    \item It evaluates cross-setting generalization by testing whether models trained in one educational setting remain reliable in another; if an independent classroom dataset becomes available, this step will be extended to external transfer-learning validation (RQ4).
\end{itemize}}

\section{Dataset Description}

The source material is the open Belgian classroom dataset with 6{,}834 satisfaction assessments from 321 occupants across 11 classrooms in three educational settings: primary school, secondary school, and a university test lecture room \cite{carton2026dataset}. It was collected in Flanders, Belgium: in three secondary-school classrooms in Haacht from April to June 2021, seven primary-school classrooms in five schools from October to December 2021, and a test lecture room at KU Leuven Ghent Campus from November 2021 to May 2022 \cite{carton2026dataset}. The secondary-school campaign covered intermediate-season conditions, the primary-school campaign covered heating-season conditions, and the test lecture room covered heating and intermediate-season conditions \cite{carton2026dataset}.

Occupant feedback was collected through repeated right-here-right-now online surveys. The surveys recorded five-point satisfaction votes for thermal, indoor-air-quality, acoustic, visual, and overall IEQ conditions, together with personal and contextual information such as occupant ID, age, gender, clothing, seating position, survey moment, activity, and classroom or group; each vote was linked to the nearest environmental measurements in time \cite{carton2026dataset}.

The environmental monitoring setup differed between the three campaigns. In the secondary school, three Renson Sense IEQ devices were placed in each classroom and measured air temperature, relative humidity, CO$_2$, light intensity, and sound level at 5-minute intervals; outdoor temperature was measured separately \cite{carton2026dataset}. In the primary schools, classroom IEQ was monitored using HOBO-MX, HOBO U12-012, and Netatmo smart weather station devices, also at 5-minute intervals, with an additional CO$_2$ monitoring device in each classroom to inform pupils and teachers about IAQ conditions \cite{carton2026dataset}. In the test lecture room, building sensors and a weather station measured indoor and outdoor environmental conditions at 1-minute intervals, an Ethera NEMo IAQ sensor measured IAQ variables at 10-minute intervals, and a Testo 480 thermal comfort meter measured additional thermal variables such as air velocity and mean radiant temperature \cite{carton2026dataset}.

This study uses the combined simplified version of the dataset, which keeps variables available across the three cases. It includes the original five-point overall IEQ satisfaction vote and a common set of environmental, occupant, and contextual variables, such as air temperature, relative humidity, CO$_2$, illuminance, LAeq, clothing, age, gender, seating position, classroom type, and survey timing.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Prediction Task and Model Inputs}

Before imputation, the model input and output variables were defined explicitly. The output variable was the overall IEQ satisfaction vote. The input variables were grouped into sensor-based measurements (\texttt{Temperature}, \texttt{RH}, \texttt{CO2}, \texttt{Lighting}, \texttt{Sound}, and \texttt{Ttrend}), occupant descriptors (\texttt{Age}, \texttt{Gender}, \texttt{Student}, and \texttt{CLO}), seating context (classroom location), and other contextual variables (\texttt{EA}, \texttt{Vote hour}, \texttt{Vote weekday}, \texttt{Moment}, and \texttt{AC on/off}). In the model-ready dataset, classroom location was represented by five binary columns: \texttt{LocationBack}, \texttt{LocationFront}, \texttt{LocationLeft}, \texttt{LocationRight}, and \texttt{LocationMiddle}. Almost all variables that were consistently available in the combined simplified dataset were retained. The remaining variables were excluded mainly because they were not available across the three cases, had too many missing values for a comparable model, or would introduce leakage. In particular, domain-specific satisfaction votes were not used as predictors because they are subjective responses close to the target variable, while trace and grouping identifiers, including \texttt{Occupant ID}, \texttt{Group}, and \texttt{Subgroup}, were excluded to avoid identity- or classroom-specific leakage and unintended personalization to individual occupants or classrooms. The completeness of the retained inputs is summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:ieq-feature-completeness}.

\subsection{Data Cleaning and Imputation}

Missing values were filled using fixed, reproducible rules. Missing classroom position was set to Middle. Clothing insulation (CLO) was imputed by the median within each Group, with the global dataset median used as fallback. In the survey and dataset documentation, clothing is recorded as either a simple three-level category (light, neutral, warm) or a more detailed clothing description, not as a directly measured insulation value. Here, the simple categories were mapped to approximate clo values of 0.44, 0.64, and 0.81 based on standard clothing-insulation tables; these values should therefore be interpreted as representative approximations rather than observed measurements \cite{carton2026dataset,ashrae2023standard55,pythermalcomfortclothing2025}. Temperature trend, sound level, and lighting were imputed by the median within Group + Subgroup, then by Group, and finally by the global median as fallback. Age was first inferred from other records with the same Occupant ID using that occupant's median reported age; if no age was available for that ID, the fallback sequence was the median within Group + student-status, then Group, then the global median. Gender was first copied from other records with the same Occupant ID; if no gender was available for that ID, it was assigned by a seeded random draw from the gender distribution within the same Group, with the overall dataset distribution used as fallback. The original five-category overall IEQ scale (1 = very dissatisfied, 2 = dissatisfied, 3 = ok, 4 = satisfied, 5 = very satisfied) was collapsed into three classes for modeling purposes, with categories 1 and 2 merged as dissatisfied, category 3 retained as neutral, and categories 4 and 5 merged as satisfied. The distributions on both scales are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:overall-ieq-distribution}.

\subsection{Model Validation Strategy}

Previous IEQ reviews show that differences in measurement protocols, building contexts, sample sizes, and modeling approaches make direct comparison between studies difficult \cite{heinzerling2013,zhao2023,roumi2022}. To ensure a fair comparison, the same validation protocol was applied to all model families.

Model performance was evaluated using five-fold cross-validation on the cleaned dataset with the defined predictor set and three-class IEQ satisfaction target. The folds were kept identical for all model families and tuning stages. Preprocessing was fitted within each training fold to prevent information leakage. Because the three target classes were unevenly distributed, macro F1 was used as the primary selection metric: it averages class-level F1 scores without weighting them by class frequency, allowing minority classes to contribute equally to model evaluation \cite{sokolova2009performance}. Accuracy is reported only as a secondary metric because it can be inflated by the dominant satisfied class. Balanced accuracy provides a complementary class-imbalance diagnostic. Ordinal mean absolute error (MAE) was also reported by coding the ordered classes as dissatisfied = 0, neutral = 1, and satisfied = 2, so larger class-distance errors were penalized more strongly than adjacent-class errors.

Macro F1 will also be used as the main metric for personalization and transfer analysis. Personalization will compare the pooled model with classroom- and occupant-specific variants and estimate how many feedback votes are needed for useful adaptation. Transfer learning will first evaluate the Belgian classroom model on a new classroom or school from an independent dataset. It will then test whether a limited number of local feedback votes improves performance in the target setting.}

\Reviewed{
\subsection{Benchmark Model Training}

The benchmark compared five supervised model families for tabular classification: Logistic Regression, Gradient Boosting, ANN/MLP, Random Forest, and Extra Trees. All five model families were evaluated on the same predictor set, cross-validation folds, and primary selection metric. Hyperparameters were tuned for each model family before the final benchmark comparison.}

\Reviewed{
\subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning and Final Model Optimization}

Hyperparameter tuning was treated as part of the modeling protocol rather than as a result. The workflow proceeded in three steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Tune all five model families using the same cross-validation protocol and macro F1 objective.
    \item Select the strongest tuned model family as the main reference model.
    \item Test additional optimization strategies for the selected model.
\end{enumerate}}

\Reviewed{After the strongest tuned model was selected, additional optimization strategies were tested for that model. These included manual temperature and CO$_2$ threshold features, ensemble and ordinal alternatives, complete-case row filtering, and synthetic class balancing. The selected Extra Trees model was also interpreted using complementary feature-importance approaches, including model-based importance, permutation importance, and SHAP-based importance grouped back to the original input variables. These analyses were used to identify potentially useful predictors for monitoring and decision support, not to claim causal effects.}
\Reviewed{
\subsection{Follow-Up Analyses}

\subsubsection{Personalization Analysis}

Personalization will be evaluated for new classrooms and new occupants. For each target classroom or occupant, all of its records will be excluded from the base model training. A limited number of target feedback votes will then be used for adaptation, and the remaining target votes will be used for testing. Learning-curve experiments will estimate how many feedback votes are needed before personalization becomes useful. Identifiers such as \texttt{Occupant ID}, \texttt{Group}, and \texttt{Subgroup} will be used only to define personalization groups and evaluation splits, not as predictors in the general model.}

\Reviewed{
\subsubsection{Transfer-Learning and Cross-Setting Validation}

Transfer learning will be evaluated using the Norwegian classroom dataset used by Alam et al.~\cite{alam2025schoolieq}. The model will first be trained on the full Belgian classroom dataset. Before evaluation, the variables and target classes will be harmonized across the Belgian and Norwegian datasets. The Belgian-trained model will then be tested on the Norwegian data before and after adaptation with a limited number of local feedback votes.}

\Reviewed{
\subsection{Extra Trees Fine Tuning Results}

Several optimization experiments were compared with the selected all-school Extra Trees model:

\paragraph{Manual temperature and CO$_2$ feature engineering.} We added three threshold indicators: CO$_2$ $>$ 1000 ppm, $T < 18^\circ$C, and $T > 24^\circ$C. Results: macro F1 = -0.003, accuracy = -0.004, balanced accuracy = -0.001, and ordinal MAE = +0.004. The indicators therefore did not improve the current baseline.

\paragraph{Imputation-indicator columns.} We added imputation flags for incomplete predictors plus a row-level imputation count. Results: macro F1 = +0.013, accuracy = +0.007, balanced accuracy = +0.015, and ordinal MAE = -0.008. This was the strongest simple improvement.

\paragraph{Soft-voting ensemble.} We combined Extra Trees and Random Forest probabilities; the best weight used 80\% Extra Trees and 20\% Random Forest. Results: macro F1 = +0.001, accuracy = 0.000, balanced accuracy = +0.001, and ordinal MAE = +0.000. The gain was negligible.

\paragraph{Ordinal classification experiment.} We tested two-threshold ordinal decompositions for $y >$ dissatisfied and $y >$ neutral. Extra Trees results: macro F1 = -0.005, accuracy = +0.008, balanced accuracy = -0.007, and ordinal MAE = -0.004. This stayed supplementary because macro F1 did not improve.

\paragraph{Data-quality and row-filtering strategies.} We tested complete-case filters for originally observed sensor values. The main-sensor filter kept 5{,}349 rows. Results: macro F1 = +0.013, accuracy = -0.002, balanced accuracy = +0.015, and ordinal MAE = +0.001. The stricter Lighting + Sound + Ttrend filter kept 2{,}314 rows. Results: macro F1 = +0.010, accuracy = -0.022, balanced accuracy = +0.011, and ordinal MAE = +0.018. We did not keep these filters because accuracy did not improve and other indicators improved only slightly.


\paragraph{Synthetic class balancing.} We tried to reduce the class imbalance by adding extra copies of under-represented training samples. This was done only inside each training fold, so the validation data remained unchanged. The best setting raised the smaller classes to 50\% of the majority class size. It slightly improved macro F1 +0.003 and balanced accuracy +0.011, but reduced accuracy -0.004 and worsened ordinal MAE +0.008, so it was not retained.

\paragraph{Class-weight and decision-threshold tuning.} We screened class weights and probability multipliers. The best class-weight setting, \{dissatisfied: 3, neutral: 2, satisfied: 1\}, gave: macro F1 = +0.006, accuracy = +0.013, balanced accuracy = -0.015, and ordinal MAE = -0.018. Nested threshold tuning gave: macro F1 = +0.008, accuracy = -0.001, balanced accuracy = +0.001, and ordinal MAE = -0.003. The gain was useful but small.

\paragraph{Ten-fold validation check.} We reran the baseline and imputation-indicator models with ten stratified folds. The ten-fold baseline reached macro F1 = 0.597, accuracy = 0.726, balanced accuracy = 0.598, and ordinal MAE = 0.311. Imputation indicators gave: macro F1 = +0.012, accuracy = +0.007, balanced accuracy = +0.015, and ordinal MAE = -0.009. Overall, the ten-fold validation produced slightly better results than the five-fold validation.

\section{Preliminary Literature Benchmark}

This section compares the final results with related IEQ prediction studies. The final Extra Trees model reached macro F1 = 0.621, accuracy = 0.737, balanced accuracy = 0.624, and ordinal MAE = 0.295 for the three-class overall IEQ target. These values are lower than some reported benchmarks, but the comparison is limited. This study predicts individual classroom votes across primary, secondary, and university classrooms, and it mainly uses sensor, occupant, and context variables rather than subjective votes from other IEQ domains.

Higher scores in the literature are often reported for easier or different settings, such as binary targets, aggregated dissatisfaction rates, person- or building-specific models, or models using subjective thermal, air-quality, visual, and acoustic satisfaction as inputs \cite{tang2022,zhao2023}. In this context, the current performance appears realistic. The model is weaker for dissatisfied and neutral votes, but it still shows that sensor and context data can support broad IEQ satisfaction screening. Macro F1 and balanced accuracy are therefore important alongside accuracy, because they show the weaker performance on minority classes.}

\section{Future Work}
Future work should move the model closer to real building operation. A practical next step would be to test it in a classroom monitoring dashboard, where predicted satisfaction could be compared with new student feedback and used to identify problematic rooms, lessons, or operating conditions. Such testing would show whether the model is useful not only as a retrospective benchmark, but also as a simple decision-support tool for school operation.

Future studies should also compare ML-based IEQ approaches with more conservative building-operation strategies. This would help determine whether IEQ satisfaction models can bring measurable energy benefits when used as part of more complex building control, and what benefits different satisfaction-energy trade-offs could provide. Further progress will require more data from different building types, schools, seasons, and climate zones. It would also be useful to define a more unified set of variables that should be collected in IEQ studies, so that future datasets are easier to combine and compare.
